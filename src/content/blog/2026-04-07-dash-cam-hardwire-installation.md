# Dash Cam Hardwire Installation

Hardwire installed front, single channel dash cam. Replaced with dual channel version and installed rear dash cam. First hands-on work with my car: faced many challenges and learned a lot along the way.

## Why I Started This Project

Dash cams are important for preventing insurance fraud, legally protecting yourself during accidents, and capturing other incidents on the road. Around [30%](https://www.autoinsurance.com/research/dash-cam-usage-report/) of American drivers now use them as of 2026.

I had never worked on my car before, and professional installers were charging $250–$300 for a front-only install and closer to $400 for front and rear. Doing it myself would be a valuable learning experience and save me real money. The goal: a fully functional dual-channel install under the $300 budget.

## Constraints
- Budget: $300
- Time: No hard deadline, preferably within a weekend or two
- Actual time spent: 8 hrs (Day 1, front), 2 hrs (Day 2, front), 10 hrs (Day 3, rear)

### ChrisFix Installation Steps (Reference Summary)

1. Use owner's manual to locate fuse box, cross-check with fuse box diagram
2. Identify battery positive wire (always 12V), ACC wire (12V only when car is on), and ground
3. With car off and multimeter grounded to bare metal, find a fuse that always reads 12V
4. Avoid fuses protecting critical systems (brakes, airbags, engine management)
5. Remove fuse, insert into fuse tap's input slot with a matching amperage fuse in the output slot
6. Install fuse tap assembly, verify 12V with car off
7. Repeat for an ACC fuse (0V off, 12V on), connect ACC wire
8. Attach ground wire ring terminal to a bare metal bolt near the fuse box
9. Plug in dash cam and verify it powers on and stays on
10. Mount behind rearview mirror: run wipers first to find the cleaned zone, connect to phone app to check framing, clean with isopropyl alcohol before sticking
11. Tuck wire above the headliner toward the A-pillar
12. Remove weatherstripping, pry A-pillar trim, zip-tie dash cam wire to factory wire harness alongside airbag module
13. Bundle and cut excess zip ties, tuck behind fuse box, reinstall cover
14. Set dash cam low-voltage cutoff to 11.6V for parking mode protection

## Process Walkthrough

### Step 1: Research and Selection

Watched the Linus Tech Tips dash cam comparison video to narrow down brands, then reviewed Viofo's documentation to confirm specs. Decided on the **Viofo A119 Mini 2** for the front install. Watched ChrisFix's hardwire installation video to understand the process and determine what parts to buy.

---

### Step 2: Front Camera Installation

#### Fuse Box Access and Circuit Testing

Located the driver-side fuse box (lower left of dashboard) and removed the cover. Grounded the multimeter's black probe to bare chassis metal, then used the red probe to test fuses with the car off. Identified a constant 12V circuit and an ACC circuit (0V off, 12V on), cross-referencing the fuse box diagram to avoid critical systems.

**Challenge:** The plastic fuse removal tool included with the fuse tap kit was too flimsy and nearly broke under pressure.

**Solution:** Found a heavy-duty OEM fuse removal tool integrated into the engine bay fuse box cover, much more durable and worked perfectly.

#### Fuse Tap Installation

**Challenge:** The standard fuse taps from the initial Amazon purchase were physically too large. My 2025 Hyundai Kona N Line uses low-profile mini fuses, not standard ATO/ATC fuses.

**Solution:** Bought the correct low-profile mini fuse taps from O'Reilly Auto Parts after physically comparing fuse dimensions in-store.

**Challenge:** After installing the fuse tap with only one fuse, the multimeter read 0V.

**Root Cause:** Fuse taps require fuses in both slots: one for the original vehicle circuit and one for the new dash cam circuit. Without both, the circuit is incomplete.

**Solution:** Inserted matching amperage fuses in both slots. Confirmed 12.3V with car off for battery positive wire, 0V with car off for ACC wire, and 12V+ with car on for ACC wire.

#### Ground Connection

Located a suitable ground bolt near the fuse box on bare chassis metal. Loosened it with ratchet set (without fully removing), slipped the hardwire kit's ring terminal over the bolt, and retightened. Verified stable voltage at the hardwire kit output.

#### Dash Cam Mounting

Ran wiper fluid and wipers to identify the cleaned zone, then used the Viofo phone app to preview framing before committing. Cleaned the windshield with isopropyl alcohol, mounted the camera behind the rearview mirror, and let the adhesive cure for 24 hours.

#### Cable Routing: Headliner and A-Pillar

Tucked the cable into the gap between the headliner and windshield edge using plastic trim tools, working toward the A-pillar without creasing the headliner.

**Challenge:** Safely removing the A-pillar trim to route wire behind it. The 2025 Kona N Line has a curtain airbag in the A-pillar. Conflicting information online about whether it uses clips or threaded fasteners, and no clear model-specific documentation, made full removal too risky to attempt.

**Solution:** Popped the trim away from its clips without fully removing it, then used a long zip tie as a fishing tool to guide the wire into the pillar channel alongside the factory wiring harness. Secured with small zip ties, confirmed no wires crossed the airbag deployment path, and reseated the trim and weatherstripping.

#### Wire Connection and Cleanup

**Challenge:** The hardwire kit's factory shrink wrap terminals couldn't be properly heat-shrunk with a lighter, leaving loose, unreliable connections.

**Solution:** Removed the shrink wrap entirely with flush cutters, stripped ~10mm of insulation from each wire end, tightly twisted the copper strands together, and wrapped with multiple layers of electrical tape under tension. Bundled all excess wire with zip ties and tucked behind the fuse box panel.

---

### Step 3: Rear Camera Installation

For the rear install, switched to **Viofo A229 Plus**. Starting from the front dash cam, the wire had to travel the full length of the vehicle:

1. **Routed through the other A-pillar**: using the same improvised zip-tie fishing method developed during the front install
2. **Removed weatherstripping and trim panels**: along the B-pillar and rear passenger side
3. **Ran wire through the C-pillar**: the weatherstripping on the trunk side was partially glued in, making removal difficult. Initially spent time researching whether there were hidden screws, it was just stiff clips and glue. Getting the wire through the internal channel was the most time-consuming part of the whole project, having a second person helped thread it through
4. **Terminated at the rear glass**: ran the wire to the top of the trunk opening. Briefly bought a USB-C extension cable at Walmart thinking more length was needed, but realized the reference video was shot on a Prius with a slanted trunk requiring a longer run. On a crossover like the Kona, the wire terminates right at the top of the trunk opening and reaches the rear glass perfectly.
5. **Mounted the rear camera**: verified live footage in the app before final adhesion

### Step 4: Validation and Testing

After each major milestone, verified:
- Dash cam powers on within 5 seconds of ignition
- Continuous recording confirmed in app (front and rear)
- No visible wires from any seating position
- All vehicle systems functioning normally (no warning lights)
- Parking mode active with low-voltage cutoff at 11.6V

## What Went Wrong

**Issue 1: Dash Cam "Powers Off" After 30 Seconds**
- **Symptom:** Dash cam turned on, then shut off exactly 30 seconds later
- **Root Cause:** Not a hardware issue, the display's screen saver was set to 30 seconds by default, making it appear the camera was shutting off. Recording was continuous the entire time.
- **Fix:** Settings → Display → Screen Saver → changed from 30s to 3 minutes

**Issue 2: Fuse Removal Tool Too Weak**
- **Symptom:** Included plastic fuse tool flexed under pressure and nearly snapped
- **Root Cause:** Aftermarket kit tools are often cheaply made
- **Fix:** Used the heavy-duty OEM fuse removal tool built into the engine bay fuse box cover

**Issue 3: Wrong Fuse Tap Size**
- **Symptom:** Fuse tap physically would not fit into fuse socket
- **Root Cause:** Vehicle uses low-profile mini fuses, purchased standard ATO fuse taps
- **Fix:** Purchased correct low-profile mini fuse taps from O'Reilly

**Issue 4: Zero Voltage from Fuse Tap**
- **Symptom:** Multimeter read 0V after installing fuse tap with one fuse
- **Root Cause:** Fuse taps require fuses in both the input and output slots to complete the circuit
- **Fix:** Added matching fuse to the output slot, confirmed 12.3V

**Issue 5: Shrink Wrap Connections Failing**
- **Symptom:** Shrink wrap wouldn't contract properly with a lighter, connections slid apart under tension
- **Root Cause:** Lighters provide uneven heat, shrink wrap remained loose
- **Fix:** Removed shrink wrap entirely, twisted copper conductors together, wrapped with multiple layers of electrical tape under tension
